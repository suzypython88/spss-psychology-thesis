## BSc Psychology (Trinity College, Dublin, 2011) - Final Year Thesis
### "Grapheme-colour synaesthesia may not improve performance in tests of spelling ability."

**Abstract:**
Grapheme-colour synaesthesia is a familial condition in which letters, numbers, days of the week and months of the year elicit colours overlaid on a printed page or in the mind’s eye. The present study implemented novel methodologies to explore memory and visual search faculties (both involved in spelling) in nine grapheme-colour synaesthetes and thirty-two non- synaesthete controls from an undergraduate population, aiming to address contradictory findings in the synaesthesia literature. Participants completed a word recognition task following the learning of 27 non-words, and a letter search task to identify target letters amongst common English words. A series of Mann-Whitney U tests found no significant differences between the groups for speed or percentage accuracy in either task, flouting expectations that synaesthesia would provided distinctive colour markers to aid performance. Correlational analyses did not reveal any relationship between the number of unique synaesthetic colours and scores in either task; nor did a majority of synaesthetes report the active use of these colours to aid memory. These findings indicate that synaesthetic colours may not provide any significant advantage in spelling ability, either in vocabulary learning or in spelling accuracy.
